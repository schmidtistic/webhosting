The Gift of a Letter

(Taken from an article written by W. H. Parachin)

When Ronald Reagan was president, he received a painful letter from a young woman in Georgia. She described a time she had been married to a Vietnam veteran who suffered terribly from flashbacks because of the war. Although he tried to get help by seeing a succession of counselors and therapists, he could not find a way to exorcise the demons that raged within.

Eventually, her husband committed suicide.

Ronald Reagan’s immediate reply was memorable and stirring. He wrote, “I am truly sorry for the tragedy you have described. I realize you feel you’ve lost everything that conflict cost you. You mention having difficulty with bitterness and anger. Those are such common reactions to suffering. I hope you can find some comfort in knowing that your grief and anger are understandable.”

This letter, with its sensitivity and empathy, gave the woman a sense that her pain was acknowledged and understood. It did not offer quick answers or easy solutions. Instead, it conveyed compassion.

A letter can be a gift.

A letter can offer encouragement. A letter can say what might be too difficult to speak aloud. A letter can affirm. A letter can comfort.

Long after the spoken word is forgotten, a letter can be read again and again.

Rules of Life

Authors Unknown

(Different quotes Grandpa often sent in letters)

Yesterday is history.
Tomorrow is a mystery.
Today is a gift.
That is why it is called the present.

I asked God to take away my pain.
God said, “No. It is not for me to take away, but for you to give it up.”

I asked God to make my handicapped child whole.
God said, “No. His spirit is whole. His body is only temporary.”

I asked God to grant me patience.
God said, “No. Patience is a byproduct of tribulations. It isn’t granted; it is earned.”

I asked God to give me happiness.
God said, “No. I give blessings. Happiness is up to you.”

I asked God to spare me pain.
God said, “No. Suffering draws you apart from worldly cares and brings you closer to me.”

I asked God to make my spirit grow.
God said, “No. You must grow on your own, but I will prune you to make you fruitful.”

I asked God for all things that I might enjoy life.
God said, “No. I will give you life, so that you may enjoy all things.”

I asked God to help me love others as much as he loves me.
God said, “Ahhhh… finally you have the idea.”

Yesterday, Today and Tomorrow

There are two days in every week that we should not worry about, two days that should be kept free from fear and apprehension.

One of these days is Yesterday.

Yesterday, with its cares and burdens, its faults and mistakes, its pains and aches.

Yesterday has passed forever beyond our control. All the money in the world cannot bring yesterday back. We cannot undo a single act or erase a single word we said. Yesterday is gone.

The other day we should not worry about is Tomorrow.

Tomorrow, with its possible adversities, its burdens, its large promise and poor performance.

Tomorrow is beyond our immediate control. Tomorrow’s sun will rise, either in splendor or behind a mask of clouds.

But whether it rises in splendor or behind clouds, we cannot control tomorrow.

Instead, let us live one day at a time.

The only day we have control over is Today.

Today, with its opportunities and responsibilities, its chances and its choices.

Today is the day to work, today is the day to love, today is the day to live.

Today is the day we can influence, shape, and make meaningful.

So let us not worry about Yesterday, for it is gone.

Let us not worry about Tomorrow, for it is not yet here.

Let us live for Today.

Heaven’s Grocery Store

I was walking down life’s highway a long time ago. One day I saw a sign that read, “Heaven’s Grocery Store.”

As I got a little closer, the door came open wide, and when I came to myself, I was standing inside.

I saw a host of angels. They were standing everywhere. One handed me a basket and said, “My child, shop with care.”

Everything a Christian needed was in that grocery store, and all you couldn’t carry you could come back for more.

First I got some patience. Love was in the same row. Further down was understanding, you need that everywhere you go.

I got a box of wisdom, a bag or two of faith. I couldn’t miss the Holy Spirit, for He was all over the place.

I stopped to get some strength and courage to help me run the race. My basket was getting full, but I remembered I still needed grace.

I didn’t forget salvation, for salvation was free. I tried to get enough of that to save both you and me.

Then I started to the counter to pay my grocery bill. I thought everything I had would surely cost me dear.

When I got up to the counter, I saw a sign that said, “All things are free.”

I smiled and said, “Thank you,” and left the store with joy. I knew I had been blessed in a way I could not destroy.

I asked for health, that I might do greater things.

I was given infirmity, that I might do better things.

Old Songs

The following are songs remembered, sung, and shared over the years. Some are listed by title, others include remembered verses as they were written.

This transcription reflects the original document as closely as possible. Minor wording differences may exist.

Amazing Grace, how sweet the sound, that saved a wretch like me. I once was lost, but now am found, was blind, but now I see.

Blessed assurance, Jesus is mine! Oh, what a foretaste of glory divine! Heir of salvation, purchase of God, born of His Spirit, washed in His blood.

What a friend we have in Jesus, all our sins and griefs to bear. What a privilege to carry everything to God in prayer.

Rock of Ages, cleft for me, let me hide myself in Thee.

In the garden I come to Thee, while the dew is still on the roses. And the voice I hear falling on my ear, the Son of God discloses.

How great Thou art, how great Thou art.

These songs were sung in homes, churches, and gatherings, and carried forward through memory as much as melody.

A Little Family History

This section records family history as it was remembered and written, reflecting personal recollection rather than formal genealogy.

This transcription is best-effort and may contain minor wording differences from the original document.

The Schmidt family roots reach back through earlier generations, shaped by hard work, faith, and perseverance.

Family members worked the land, raised livestock, and built homes with their own hands.

Life was not easy, but necessities were provided through effort, cooperation, and responsibility.

Education was valued, even when opportunities were limited.

Children were taught the importance of honesty, respect for others, and commitment to family.

Faith played an important role in daily life, guiding decisions and providing strength in difficult times.

The family endured periods of hardship, including economic uncertainty and times of loss.

Through these challenges, family bonds remained strong.

Traditions were passed down through stories, shared work, music, and gatherings.

Each generation contributed in its own way, building on the work and sacrifices of those before them.

This history is remembered not as a list of dates, but as a record of character, effort, and continuity.

A Letter to All the Great Grandchildren

This letter was written with the future in mind, addressed to generations not yet grown.

This transcription is best-effort and may contain minor wording differences from the original document.

Dear Great Grandchildren,

I am writing this letter to you because I want you to know something about your family and where you came from.

You may be living in a very different time than I did, with new inventions, new ways of doing things, and new challenges to face.

No matter how much the world changes, some things never change.

Work hard at whatever you do. Do not expect something for nothing.

Be honest in your dealings with others. Your name and reputation are worth more than money.

Treat people fairly and with respect, no matter who they are or where they come from.

Take care of your family. Family is one of the greatest blessings you will ever have.

Help others whenever you can. You never know how much a small kindness may mean.

Learn to accept responsibility for your actions. Mistakes will happen, but what matters is how you respond to them.

Do not be afraid of hard times. Difficult experiences can teach you the most important lessons.

Be thankful for what you have. Gratitude will carry you through both good times and bad.

Remember those who came before you. Their work and sacrifices helped make your life possible.

If you remember these things, you will be able to face life with confidence and purpose.

I hope that when you read this, you will know that you were loved and thought of long before you were born.

With love,

Your Great Grandfather
